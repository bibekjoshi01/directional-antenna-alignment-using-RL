\subsection{Hardware Design}

The hardware architecture was designed to support continuous azimuth rotation,
independent elevation control, and stable RSSI sampling under motor actuation.
Emphasis was placed on mechanical rigidity, electrical noise isolation, and
modular separation between power, logic, and actuation subsystems.

\subsubsection{Mechanical Configuration and Structural Design}

The physical architecture is organized into a vertical stack consisting of a Power Deck, a Logic Deck, and a rotating Top Floor.

\textbf{Stationary Base (Power \& Logic Decks):} The lower foundation, designated as the Power Deck, houses the energy source consisting of a 3S Li-Ion battery pack. This deck features a dovetail rail mechanism to facilitate easy removal and maintenance of the batteries. Power is routed through a Battery Management System (BMS) to a female XT60 connector. Above this sits the Logic Deck, which encloses the primary azimuth actuator, a NEMA 17 bipolar stepper motor. This motor bears the structural load of the rotating upper assembly.

\textbf{Rotating Turret:} The upper rotating platform acts as the mounting chassis for
the main controller (ESP32-S3), the elevation actuator (MG90S servo), and the antenna
system. The azimuth motion of this turret is produced by the NEMA 17 stepper motor
located in the stationary base, while the servo mounted on the turret provides the
local tilt adjustment of the antenna. To address mechanical instability, a coupling was
employed to interface the motor shaft with the PCB, providing a stable, level plane for
the rotor electronics.

\subsubsection{Electromechanical Interface}
To enable continuous 360-degree rotation without cable entanglement, a custom PCB-based slip ring was fabricated. 
This interface manages the transmission of power and logic signals across the rotating boundary.
A PCB-based slip ring was selected over commercial capsule slip rings to reduce
cost, allow custom signal routing, and enable tight integration with the system
mechanical envelope. The design also permits easy modification of ring width and
spacing to improve contact reliability during iterative prototyping.


\textbf{Stator (Base Side):} The stator consists of a copper-clad board etched with four concentric conductive rings. These rings correspond to the required transmission channels: 5V power, ground,
and digital control signals (STEP and DIR) for the azimuth motor driver.

\textbf{Rotor (Top Side):} The rotating contact mechanism utilizes spring-loaded pogo pins. These are arranged in redundant pairs for each ring to ensure continuous electrical continuity even if one pin momentarily disconnects due to mechanical vibration.

\subsubsection{Electronic Control and Circuit Design}

The system is powered by a 3S Li-Ion battery pack providing a nominal 12V supply. This 12V rail is used directly to drive the stepper motor through the A4988 driver located on the stationary base. A regulated 5V supply is derived and transmitted through the PCB-based slip ring to power the ESP32-S3 and the elevation servo on the rotating platform.

\textbf{Power Distribution:} The 5V line and common ground are routed through dedicated slip ring tracks. Local decoupling capacitors are placed near the ESP32-S3 and servo supply pins to stabilize voltage under dynamic load and mitigate fluctuations caused by variable slip ring contact resistance.

\textbf{Motor Driver Integration:} The A4988 stepper driver is mounted on the base, in close proximity to the NEMA 17 motor, to keep high-current paths short. Motor supply lines are not routed through the slip ring, reducing electrical noise, contact wear, and voltage drop across the rotating interface.

\textbf{Azimuth Control Implementation:} STEP and DIR control signals are generated by
the ESP32-S3 on the rotating turret and routed through the slip ring to the A4988
driver. These signals are carried over low-current tracks, with routing kept minimal to
reduce susceptibility to noise introduced at the rotating interface.

\textbf{Elevation Control Implementation:} The MG90S servo is directly connected to the
ESP32-S3 on the rotor. A dedicated PWM-capable GPIO pin is used, with short signal
routing and local decoupling at the servo terminals to suppress switching noise during
operation. Because the tilt actuator is located entirely on the rotating section, no
tilt-control signal needs to cross the slip ring.

\textbf{Microcontroller Integration:} The ESP32-S3 is mounted on the rotor PCB and serves as the central control unit for all rotating components. It is powered via the slip ring 5V supply, with local filtering to ensure stable operation. PCB layout considerations include separation of power and signal traces, use of wider traces for supply lines, and provision of connectors for modular assembly and debugging.
