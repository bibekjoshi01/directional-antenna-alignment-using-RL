\subsection{Experimental Setup and Evaluation}

This section defines the experimental setup used to evaluate the autonomous antenna
alignment system, the metrics considered for assessment, and the protocol used to
compare the RL-based controller with the baseline methods. The goal is to ensure that
all algorithms are tested under the same initial conditions, actuator limits, and RSSI
measurement pipeline.

\subsubsection{Experimental Environment}

The system is tested in two primary environments to assess behavior under varying
propagation conditions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Indoor environment:} Laboratory or corridor settings with
    reflective surfaces such as walls and metallic objects. These conditions introduce
    multipath propagation and noticeable RSSI fluctuation.
    \item \textbf{Outdoor environment:} Open or semi-open areas with transmitter and
    receiver separation in the range of 10--30~m during practical trials. These
    conditions provide stronger line-of-sight behavior while still allowing mild
    obstruction scenarios.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Experiments are conducted under the following conditions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The transmitter orientation and transmit power are kept fixed throughout a
    trial set.
    \item The receiver starts from a deliberately misaligned orientation selected from
    the same predefined set for all compared algorithms.
    \item Static runs are performed without intentional disturbance.
    \item Disturbed runs are performed by introducing a controlled angular offset or a
    short temporary obstruction after initial alignment.
    \item Multiple trials are repeated for each environment and disturbance condition.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}

To enable quantitative and fair comparison of RL-based alignment against baseline
methods, the following metrics are recorded:

\textbf{Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI):} 
The primary metric for assessing link quality. For each decision step, the reported
RSSI is the average of 20 consecutive packet-level measurements acquired with a
10~ms inter-sample delay.

\textbf{Alignment Accuracy ($A_{acc}$):} 
Measures how close the final orientation selected by an algorithm is to the reference
orientation obtained by exhaustive scanning over the same practical search region:
\begin{equation}
A_{acc} = \left| \theta_{\text{alg}} - \theta_{\text{ref}} \right| +
\left| \phi_{\text{alg}} - \phi_{\text{ref}} \right|
\end{equation}
where $(\theta_{\text{ref}}, \phi_{\text{ref}})$ denotes the azimuth-tilt pair that
produces the highest averaged RSSI during the reference exhaustive scan, and
$(\theta_{\text{alg}}, \phi_{\text{alg}})$ is the final orientation selected by the
algorithm under test.

\textbf{Convergence Time ($T_{conv}$):} 
Time required for the algorithm to reach a stable alignment such that:
\begin{equation}
|RSSI(t) - RSSI_{\text{ref}}| \le \epsilon, \quad \forall t \in [T_{\text{conv}}, T_{\text{conv}}+\tau]
\end{equation}
where $RSSI_{\text{ref}}$ is the reference maximum averaged RSSI measured by the
exhaustive scan, $\epsilon = 2$~dB is the acceptance tolerance, and $\tau$ is a short
verification window of four control cycles.

\textbf{Link Stability ($S$):} 
Variance of RSSI after convergence:
\begin{equation}
S = \text{Var}(RSSI(t)), \quad t \in [T_{conv}, T_{conv}+W]
\end{equation}
where $W$ corresponds to the next ten logged control cycles after convergence. Lower
variance indicates better stability and resistance to environmental noise.

\textbf{Mechanical Efficiency and Energy Proxy ($E$):} 
Indirect measure of actuator effort:
\begin{equation}
E = N_{\text{pan}} + N_{\text{tilt}}
\end{equation}
where $N_{\text{pan}}$ and $N_{\text{tilt}}$ denote the number of commanded azimuth
and tilt movements respectively. This metric is used as a simple proxy for mechanical
wear and actuation effort. Lower $E$ indicates more efficient alignment.

\subsubsection{Experimental Protocol}

All experiments follow the same protocol to ensure reproducibility:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Select an initial azimuth and tilt pair from a predefined list of
    misaligned starting states.
    \item Run each algorithm from the same starting state within the same environment.
    \item Use identical runtime parameters for RSSI sampling, actuator limits, and
    settling delay across all algorithms.
    \item Record timestamps, averaged RSSI, orientation indices, chosen actions, and
    convergence status for every control cycle.
    \item For disturbance tests, apply the same disturbance pattern across compared
    runs within a trial set.
    \item Repeat each test condition for at least 20 trials.
    \item Compare the RL-based method with the baseline approaches: fixed orientation,
    exhaustive angular scan, and RSSI-based hill-climbing.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Statistical Analysis}

For each metric ($A_{acc}, T_{conv}, S, E$), the mean and standard deviation are
computed across trials. Because the compared algorithms are run from matched starting
conditions, paired comparisons can be used where appropriate during the results stage.
This structure supports a fair and quantitative evaluation of the proposed system under
practical operating conditions.
